Court Vision — The Blueprint

July 21, 2026 · The master plan: what the machine is, how good each part honestly is, what the best in the world do, and the verified upgrade path for every piece. Written after a deep-research pass — 8 technical deep-dives, 41 research agents, 14 load-bearing recommendations adversarially fact-checked (none refuted). This document is the axe, sharpened.

The goal in one sentence: a robot that watches a tennis broadcast and writes down every shot of every point in the Match Charting Project's notation — fully automatically — with a human checking only the calls the robot itself flags as shaky.

How to read this document

This is the reference the devlog will point at, written the way the primer is written: tennis first, jargon defined on arrival, no prior post required. It has five parts:

  1. The machine as a picture — every part of the system as a box you can point at.
  2. Station by station — for each part: what it does, today's honest grade, what the best in the world do, and the upgrade.
  3. The three reframes — the research findings that change the plan.
  4. The roadmap — the upgrades in order, each one independently valuable and independently measurable.
  5. The devlog reset — the new writing contract: plain English is the post, not a box inside it.

Two honest numbers to hold onto while reading. Today, about 2 in 3 drafted points land within 5 corrections of what a human charter wrote, and the machine's "trust this one" flag is right about 9 times in 10 when it raises its hand (92% precision at 48% coverage, graded on matches it never saw). And the best academic system in the world — a university team with 114 fully annotated matches and serious GPUs — transcribes an entire point perfectly only about 40% of the time. That number matters: it says the goal of "robot charts, human reviews the flagged calls" isn't a compromise. It's what state of the art actually looks like, and the whole field designs for it.

1. The machine as a picture

One design fact worth naming, because the research kept validating it: this machine contains exactly one neural network today (the ball tracker, borrowed pretrained) and exactly one in-house learned model in the shipped path (the confidence flag). Everything else is hand-written rules. The July 20 pivot — train on the 11,646-match corpus instead of hand-tuning — is the plan to change that, and every deep-dive below independently agreed it's the right call. The corpus-trained prototypes already beat the hand rules everywhere they've been tried: serve zone 59.7% vs 31.7%, ending type 59.3% vs 22.8%.

2. Station by station: today's grade and the verified upgrade

Each part below gets the same treatment: what it does in plain English, today's honest number, what the best in the world do (from the research, sources at the bottom), and the upgrade — every one fact-checked by adversarial reviewers who tried to refute it and couldn't.

🎾 The ball tracker usable, one fatal blind spot

What it does: finds the ball in every frame, fresh each time, like a line judge who never blinks — a tiny specialist network (WASB) that has only ever looked at tennis balls. Today: solid on hard courts (~87% of frames), thin and gappy on clay (8–41% on the Roland Garros match — a fuzzy yellow ball against orange dirt is genuinely harder to see), and it reliably loses the ball just before an out-of-bounds bounce. That last one is the project's #1 confirmed blindness: it's why the machine cannot tell a ball that sailed wide from one that sailed deep, which is why point endings grade at 20%.

What the best do: two things, and the first is a genuine reframe. Hawk-Eye doesn't photograph the ball touching the ground either — nobody does. The landing spot is calculated from the flight path, the way a veteran line judge reads an arc and knows it's sailing long before it lands. Two papers at this year's top vision conference do exactly this from a single broadcast camera; one was tested on broadcast tennis and predicted landing spots to within 0.63 meters. Second, the newest trackers (TOTNet, BlurBall, TrackNetV4) handle occlusion and motion blur by design — they treat the blur streak as signal (its direction is a free speed-and-heading reading) and they don't panic when the ball briefly vanishes, because balls don't teleport.

Upgrade — verified
Stop trying to un-blink. Build the landing-spot calculator first — take the last stretch of flight the tracker did see, fit falling-ball physics to it, and read where the arc meets the ground. Day-one version: extend the arc in screen coordinates to the bounce moment, then convert just the landing point to court coordinates (the verifiers flagged that converting the whole airborne arc through the court-plane conversion is the primer's "shadow problem" — so we don't). Pure math, no GPU, testable immediately against all 8 graded matches. Then fix clay the way a hard-courter fixes clay: a short training block — a few hundred corrected clay frames, machine pre-labels, human only corrects — and fine-tune the tracker. Days of work for the calculator; the clay fine-tune is the first "weeks" project.

📐 The court fit the last big manual knob

What it does: works out the conversion between "this spot on your TV screen" and "two feet inside the baseline" — the homography from the primer. Today: a human clicks the 4 corners for every new match and eyeballs the overlay. The automatic version failed at Roland Garros (the far baseline is faint and brushed-over; the fit came back visibly skewed, 74 pixels off).

What the best do: soccer solved this for TV graphics years ago, and the recipe is consistent across every winner: don't hunt for 4 corners — recognize every line and every intersection at once (the T of the service box, the center hash, all of it), because a scuffed line still shows meters of visible paint even when its corners are invisible. Then — the important part — the system grades its own fit by drawing where it thinks the lines are and measuring the miss, and abstains when the overlay is sloppy, exactly like a player who only burns a challenge when genuinely unsure. There's even a free head start: a public tennis-court keypoint network trained on 8,841 broadcast images across hard, clay, and grass, with 1.83-pixel median error.

Upgrade — verified
Auto-initialize, self-grade, abstain. Drop the public 14-keypoint court net in as the proposer; keep the existing fit-scoring machinery (it already reprojects all nine court lines against the observed line paint — the verifier found ~70% of the needed code already in fitcourt.py) as the judge, with a calibrated accept/reject threshold learned from our 8 hand-fit matches. Below threshold → a human clicks corners, same as today. Two honest caveats from verification: the per-match color band and frame window remain as small config values (so this shrinks the knob, not deletes it outright), and the public net has no license — fine for the research phase, but the business phase retrains our own (every hand-fit match already generates free training labels). Longer term: add line-heatmap channels, the documented fix for faint clay baselines.

🕴 The player finder smudges hit their ceiling

What it does: finds the two players by comparing each frame against an empty-court photo (background subtraction — the primer's zero-dollar player detector). The forehand/backhand call is then made from which side of the player-smudge the ball arrives on. Today: that geometry has formally plateaued — counterfactual experiments proved no re-anchoring of the smudge fixes it. About 45% of shot-type errors trace to a bad or missing player box.

What the best do: draw a stick figure. Pose estimation marks wrists, elbows, shoulders, hips, and feet on each player every frame, so instead of a smudge you get an actual swing to read — take-back, contact point, follow-through. The catch: the far player is thumbnail-sized at 720p, so the stick figure gets shaky at the wrists. The 2026 answer (from the same lab as the field's tennis benchmark) is to not judge the skeleton alone: wire both skeletons, the ball, and the court corners into one connected picture, the way a commentator reads a shot from body + ball + court position at once. In the few-labels world Court Vision lives in, that keypoint-graph approach beats raw-video AI by 2–3×.

Upgrade — verified by actually running it
Skeletons this week, graph model next month. The pose swap is the single highest-leverage cheap move in this plan: pip install rtmlib, Apache-licensed, runs on this Mac — the verification agent literally installed it and measured 87 frames/second on Apple Silicon, feeding our existing player boxes in, getting 17 keypoints + confidences out. Week-one gate: eyeball far-player skeletons on all 8 matches; if wrists are garbage, fall back to shoulders/hips/feet. Then train the letters model on the corpus: a small keypoint-graph classifier (2M parameters, Mac-trainable) over skeletons + ball + corners per shot window. Handedness is not a vision problem at all — it's a lookup (MCP metadata), verified once per player by majority vote over 50 serves.

👂 The moments: hits and bounces inherits every track hole

What it does: finds each hit and bounce from kinks in the ball's path — a hit reverses the ball's direction; a bounce doesn't ("the court doesn't return serve"). Today: works where the track works; blind wherever the track has holes. Rally lengths run 57–83% within ±1 by match, and missing shots are the single largest correction bin (~2 per point).

What the best do: two independent witnesses we're not using. First, pixel-watching event spotters: small networks that look at the raw footage — the racquet blur, the ball flattening against the court — and name the exact frame of contact. The founding benchmark of this entire sub-field is broadcast tennis, its event classes are literally "serve contact, swing contact, ball bounce," the model is tiny (4.5M parameters), free (BSD license), ships pretrained tennis weights, and scores 96% at one-frame accuracy. Because it watches the whole picture instead of following the ball dot, it still sees the bounce a tracker lost. Second, ears: the "pock" of strings on ball is one of the most distinctive sounds in sport. The one published broadcast-tennis study (Australian Open footage) got useful hit detection from audio alone, and microphones don't care whether the ball left the frame. Commentary and crowd noise are the known failure modes — a day-one experiment settles how clean our feeds are.

Upgrade — verified
Give the machine ears (days), then better eyes (weeks). Audio first: extract the soundtrack (already downloaded with every match), run onset detection — the verifier ran it on a staged match in seconds of CPU — calibrate the constant audio/video offset per file, and feed hit-time candidates into the event layer as a second witness. Then run the pretrained tennis event spotter on all 8 benchmark matches, zero-shot, and score it against the current kink detector. One honest caveat from verification: a spotter answers when, not where — wide-vs-deep also needs the landing spot, which is the ball station's job (re-run the ball detector's single-frame heatmap around the spotted bounce frame, no track continuity needed). The two stations fix the blindness together: the spotter finds the bounce moment, the heatmap finds the ball in that frame, the physics extrapolator crosschecks both.

✍️ The words: from positions to notation the part nobody else has built

What it does: assembles the chart — who hit it (alternation + votes), forehand/backhand, direction, ending — and writes the MCP string. Today: a stack of hand-written rules, each with a measured ceiling: directions 72% (the strongest), letters 60%, serve zone 40%, endings 20%.

What the best do: the closest published system (F3Set, ICLR 2025 — 114 annotated broadcast matches, a vocabulary that maps almost token-for-token onto MCP's) teaches three lessons. One: predict shot elements (side, direction, technique, outcome) separately rather than one giant guess — the long tail is unlearnable otherwise. Two: after making per-shot calls, a small context refiner re-reads the whole rally and fixes calls that don't make tennis sense — a chair umpire overruling a line judge. That's a learned version of exactly the "notation prior × pixel likelihood" fusion already planned here. Three: their ceiling — 75–80% per element, ~40% per whole point — with 14× our aligned data and GPUs. The speech-recognition literature (the closest older analog: audio in, text out) adds the deciding vote: with scarce aligned data, hybrid systems (perception model + language prior fused at decode time) beat end-to-end — and Court Vision has 1.85M points of "language" data and only 8 matches of aligned video. Hybrid is not the training-wheels option; it's the correct architecture at this scale.

Upgrade — verified
One decoder to rule the words: grammar-constrained fusion. Build a small beam search that walks the MCP grammar token by token — it literally cannot write an invalid point, the way a scoring app grays out illegal entries — scoring each candidate token as what notation history expects (the corpus prior: next-shot side already at 72.9% vs a 50.9% base) plus what the pixels saw (the per-shot classifiers). ~150 lines of numpy, every token's score decomposable into its two ingredients — maximum explainability. Verification caveat, folded into the plan: the priors and pixel likelihoods don't yet exist in per-token form (side ≠ letter; letters/directions currently output decisions, not probabilities), so the honest first step is retraining the prior into per-slot form and giving each classifier a probability output. Also on the record: the end-to-end trigger — if we ever have 100+ aligned matches AND the hybrid plateaus below F3Set's per-element numbers for two consecutive benchmark rounds, we revisit. Until then, hybrid.

⚖️ The grader and the flywheel the project's superpower — mechanize it

What it does: grades every draft against human charts in token edits, and flags trustworthy points (92% precision at 48% coverage, graded on held-out matches). Today: the discipline is exceptional — frozen constants, leave-one-match-out, versioned answer keys — but it's run by hand, and the corpus's 1.85M labels aren't flowing into training because they aren't pinned to video frames.

What the best do: three patterns, all verified. First, the golden set: freeze the graded benchmark and re-run it automatically on every change, sliced by surface — so a clay regression can never hide behind hard-court wins (the verifier measured the video-free stages: the whole 8-match gate runs in under a minute, and the frozen inputs are ~29MB of coordinate CSVs — committable). Second, transcript alignment: our charts are transcripts — "serve, return, volley" — and snapping each shot to the moments the ball visibly (or audibly) changes direction converts free notation into frame-level training labels at scale. Soccer research proved this exact pattern; it's how 8 aligned matches become 30–50 without any new human labeling (clustered by broadcaster family, because score-bug crop geometry is per-feed). Third, the challenge-system loop: robot charts everything, human reviews only the flagged calls — but a varied set of them (clustered sampling), not fifty replays of the same deuce-court serve, and every correction is triple-value: it fixes the chart, trains the model, and audits the confidence flag. The review bench this loop needs is already built (review.py). Track one product number forever: human-minutes per charted match. It must fall every cycle. Later, for the business: conformal prediction (a free statistical wrapper) turns "92% observed" into "guaranteed at most X% errors in the auto-accepted set, per surface" — a claim a customer can price.

3. The three reframes

Most of the research confirmed the project's instincts. Three findings genuinely change the plan:

  1. You don't need to see the ball land. The wide-vs-deep blindness has been treated as a tracking problem — track the ball further, into the bounce. The field says: wrong frame. Hawk-Eye infers the landing spot from the flight path; the bounce moment comes from pixel/audio event spotters that don't need a ball track at all. Physics + a spotter + a single-frame heatmap replace "perfect tracking," and all three pieces are cheap.
  2. Few labels favors stick figures. With mountains of labeled video, raw-pixel AI wins. With a handful of aligned matches — our world — models that read skeletons + ball + court keypoints win by 2–3×, learn from 50–100 rallies, train on a Mac, and happen to be built from exactly the modules this pipeline already computes. The modular architecture isn't a compromise on the way to a big model; it's the sample-efficient choice.
  3. The corpus becomes training data the moment it's pinned to the clock. 11,646 human-charted matches are transcripts waiting for timestamps. Score-bug alignment (built, 74% auto) plus shot-to-event snapping (small, local, next) mints frame-level labels from free notation — the same playbook soccer research scaled. Labels were never the bottleneck; alignment was, and it's falling.

And one guardrail, confirmed with numbers: video LLMs stay out of the per-frame loop. The chatty video AIs are measurably weak at exactly what charting needs — precise moments, counting, fine-grained calls (GPT-4o: 57.8% on hard sports-video tasks) — they sample ~1 frame per second so a serve literally happens between their glances, and a single full-match pass costs $12–50 versus ~$0 local. They earn their keep only on one-off staging chores: double-checking a score-bug read, sanity-checking a court fit, describing a failure frame. Pennies, not dollars, and never in the loop.

The risk register (the business half of "hardened")

  • MCP corpus license — existential for the commercial path. The Match Charting Project is CC BY-NC-SA (non-commercial, share-alike). Research and devlog use: fine. A paid product trained on it: needs a conversation with Jeff Sackmann first — which is also simply the right conversation to have with the community this project wants to serve. Action: rights memo + outreach before any revenue.
  • Unlicensed dependencies. TennisCourtDetector (no license), F3Set (no license — email the NUS authors), TennisProject (no license). Pattern: use for research, record in each component's model card, and keep the self-trained replacement path named (our matches already generate the training data).
  • License traps avoided by choice: Ultralytics YOLO is AGPL (copyleft — ships your source with your product); Sapiens pose is non-commercial; DINOv3 is a custom Meta license. The chosen stack (WASB MIT, rtmlib Apache, E2E-Spot BSD, ByteTrack MIT, MAPIE BSD) is permissive throughout.
  • Broadcast video rights. Clips stay local, never redistributed — already policy. The product sells the notation and review service, not footage.
  • Scope contract. Singles only; standard scoring; faults, lets, challenges, and retirements are abstain-and-flag, never guess (faults are today's known miss: every draft assumes a first-serve point). Doubles is out of MVP. Each exclusion wired to a detector that raises its hand.
  • Ground-truth noise ceiling. MCP charts are single-volunteer labels; nobody knows the human-vs-human agreement rate. Some of the gap to 100% is label ambiguity, most plausibly on letters and endings. Worth measuring once two charters' versions of the same match can be compared.

5. The devlog reset: plain English IS the post

The audit read all 17 posts. The verdict, bluntly: the series has introduced ~50 technical concepts and properly explained about 10 (the ones in the primer). The body prose is written for a reader who has read every prior post and holds the repo open — codename callbacks ("M0's oldest lesson wearing a new outfit"), unglossed jargon (ECC, tophat, logit, AUC), stat barrages with shifting denominators (15/36 → 21/49), and chart strings never once decoded (s5b2f1f2f2x@ — in a series nominally for readers who don't chart). Meanwhile the Plain English boxes are the best writing in the series. The fix is not better boxes. The fix is that the box's voice becomes the whole post.

The post formula (every post, starting with cv-18)

  1. Video first with a plain-words caption — unchanged.
  2. Cold open: the day's question as one tennis-shaped sentence. ("Today the software learns to answer: who hit that ball?")
  3. "Previously": 2–4 sentences restating only the prior facts this post needs — every callback carries its own five-second recap; never a bare cv-NN or milestone number.
  4. The concept, analogy first: teach it through tennis before naming it; then name it once, bold, with a one-line definition and a primer link. One concept per post. A two-concept session is two posts, or one concept demoted to the appendix with a promise.
  5. The story: attempt → what the picture showed → what that means. One idea per sentence.
  6. The scoreboard: one small table, plain-word row labels, every number restated ("19 of 49 — call it 2 points in 5") and anchored to what good looks like.
  7. The takeaway: one sentence a tennis fan could repeat at dinner. Aphorisms are welcome after their mechanism, never before.
  8. A collapsed appendix ("For the technical reader") holding the thresholds, full scorecards, clip IDs, and API quirks that used to clog the body.
  9. Cost line and next question — unchanged.

House rules

  • Ban list for body prose: repo identifiers (point_59, serve_zone(), v1–v5), unexpanded acronyms (ECC, RLE, AUC, LOMO, HSV, bgsub), naked fraction chains, and codenames — say "the night-match test," not "t1." All welcome in the appendix.
  • Decode every notation string where it appears, token by token, in tennis words ("s5 — serve out wide; b2 — backhand up the middle; x@ — netted error"). A reusable legend goes in the primer.
  • Define on first use, every post — a returning term gets a short gloss + primer link every time, because every post is someone's first.
  • Extend the primer with the ~15 concepts that have no plain-English home yet: color masking (HSV), line detection (Hough), stabilization (ECC), residuals, F1, logit, AUC, base rate, the logistic model, counterfactual tables, parity arithmetic, and the notation legend.
  • Frontmatter last, as a headline: one sentence, ≤25 words, no stats chains — it's the reader's first contact, not the appendix's summary.
  • The acceptance test (each post's own frozen benchmark): a tennis-literate reader who has read zero prior posts must be able to say (a) what problem the post solves, (b) the day's concept in their own words, and (c) whether the result was good — using only this post and the primer. Fail any → the Previously paragraph or a gloss is missing.
  • Keep what works: video-first, honest dead ends, the cost ledger, tennis-fact-as-algorithm framing ("a live rally sends the ball across the net every shot"), and cliffhanger endings — with the setup restated next time for cold readers.

The roadmap above is also an editorial calendar, one concept per build: the Hawk-Eye principle (nobody sees the ball land). The court that fits itself (and knows when it hasn't). Stick figures beat smudges. The machine grows ears. The robot that can't write an illegal point. Turning 11,646 scorecards into a coach. The challenge-system loop. Each post teaches the concept; each build moves a number on the same frozen benchmark; the series compounds.

Receipts

Method — how this document was produced

One research workflow, 41 agents: 3 mapped the current state (all 33 code modules, all 17 posts + primer, the benchmark/scorecard/model-card docs); 8 ran technical deep-dives (ball tracking, court calibration, pose/strokes, event spotting, sequence models, training strategy, hardening, foundation models); each deep-dive's load-bearing recommendations (14 selected) were then attacked by two independent adversarial reviewers — one fact-checking every citation against fetched sources, one refuting feasibility against this repo, this Mac, and this budget (several ran the actual code to check). Verdicts: 12 evidence-CONFIRMED, 2 PLAUSIBLE, 0 refuted; feasibility caveats are folded into the plan above. A completeness critic then flagged what the deep-dives missed (full-match segmentation, identity/state machine, licensing, throughput, scope, review surface) — folded into the risk register and roadmap. This complements, and does not repeat, the July 9 commercial-landscape review (docs/landscape-2026-07.md).

Corrections this research surfaced (kept on the record)
  • TT3D's code license was misreported as CC BY-SA in the first research pass; the verifier corrected it (the paper's dataset carries the CC license). Check the repo before reuse.
  • "Drop-in court net deletes the last manual knob" was overstated — two small per-match config values (color band, frame window) survive; the knob shrinks rather than disappears.
  • The event spotter answers when, not where: wide/deep needs the landing location too, hence the paired single-frame heatmap step.
  • The fusion decoder's inputs don't exist in per-token form yet — the notation prior is three task classifiers, and letters/directions output decisions, not probabilities. Retraining them into probability form is real (days-scale) work the roadmap now owns.
  • Our own shorthand had drifted: the shipped confidence model is a logistic regression (not a random forest — that's the unshipped corpus prototype), and the score-bug OCR still lives in experiments/, not the package. Cryptic prose confuses its own author eventually; that's half the reason for section 5.
